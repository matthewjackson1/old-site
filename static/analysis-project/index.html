Abstract

Motivation

Little is known about the bacterial response to nitrogen starvation. Recent experimental work suggests some bacteria (including M.smegmatis) may be storing nitrogen in times of plenty, as they can continue to grow after it is exhausted from the medium. One possibility is that nitrogen is being stored in the form of specialised proteins, and this is what we want to investigate.

Methods

By using a random forest-based approach, we develop a classifier based on sequence-derived properties of known nitrogen storage proteins from across the domains of life. We statistically evaluate its performance and test it on several proteomes, including M.smegmatis, to try to identify putative storage protein candidates.

Results

The classifier was 90% accurate at discriminating storage proteins and non-storage proteins, and is not biased to any particular families. It identified 7 candidates at a precision level of 99% in M.smegmatis, which are worthy of further investigation.

Conclusion

The random forest approach is fast and highly effective at discriminating proteins based on sequence-based properties, and could be easily applied to similar investigations. Further work with the candidates identified in this project could help develop our understanding of nitrogen stress responses in bacteria. There are wider ramifications in the field of medicine where, if their existence is confirmed, bacterial nitrogen storage proteins could make potential antibiotic targets.
